Can E-Sports Be Evergreen like Sporting Events? Part I

By Jason Kim

The Day was August 4th, 2012.

Two young adults in what looked like driver suits lined into two separate gaming booths in the midst of dozens of pro gamers and thousands of fans who packed into Jamsil stadium, a place normally known to hold the biggest sporting events in Korea.

A somber excitement was simmering in the air, as this was the finals of the last major tournament of professional Starcraft: Brood War’s – the end of professional Starcraft I.

Several speculations have been thrown out there in regards to exactly why Starcraft I had to end – the emergence of Starcraft II as a competitor in the e-sports scene, the notorious match-fixing incident that involved hands of several players with household names.

However, of those speculations and perhaps what many regard as the sad but sobering truth of it all – the game itself has lost popularity. Gone are the days when Starcraft I dominated western sales and net cafes in Korea – the masses had moved on to other fresh, new games like League of Legends, or the plethora of soon-to-emerge console games.

And that is formula that we have known and only known as gamers - games have shelf-life. When we buy games as consumers, we expect to be entertained for a couple years, and then on to the next shiny game that our friends are playing. We expect games to die, and if they don’t die, they aren’t fresh and sexy to play.

And now is the conundrum that all current e-sport titles are facing: how long can an e-sport title live? Are the current fans and players on the clock, just waiting for the inevitable when their favorite game dies?

If then, how will the e-sport scene truly look like? People have raved about the potential of e-sport market for years, yet we have not seen a game with the potential to continue onto perpetuity like we see with football, basketball, or many other major league sports. We never hear fans of basketball complain about how their sports are getting stale, or how a new sport will replace the current versions. The game remains consistently fresh to the consumers, as the new players rejuvenate the scene while the sport remains evergreen and constant. Why couldn’t we have this mentality towards video games, instead of always demanding change when it may not be necessary?
